  <p>In the first of this tripartite series, I will provide a high-level discussion of the adminstration of income taxes in Canada. In terms of learning objectives, by the end of this article, you will understand:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Who is liable for tax</li>
    <li>How income is reported</li>
    <li>When, how, and why to file taxes</li>
  </ul>
  <p>In Canada, the collection of income taxes for the federal government and most provinces/territories falls under the purview of the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA). The CRA has the mandate to collect taxes, administer benefit programs and tax credits, in addition to enforcing Canada's numerous tax laws. While many people often think of the <i>Income Tax Act</i> as the penultimate tax Act in Canada, the CRA does also enforces taxes with respect to certain goods (under the <i>Excise Tax Act</i>), amoungst others.</p>
  <p>Contrary to popular belief, liability to pay income tax extends to all Canadians, <b>including minors</b>. For reasons that will become clear, even if an individual does not have any tax owing, it can still be adventageous to file a tax return.</p>
  <p>Broadly, the <i>Income Tax Act</i> differentiates between <b>Residents</b> and <b>Non-Residents</b>, as summarized below:<p>

  <p>As can be seen, only Residents are federally and provincially taxed on their worldwide income, while Non-Residents are only subject to federal taxes on their Canadian-source income. As a consequence, Non-Residents do not get access to most tax credits or benefits, which are reserved for only residents.</p>
  <p>It is important to note that a foreigner with a study permit <i>could</i> be considered a resident for tax purposes, while a Canadian citizen <i>could</i> also be considered a non-resident, depending on their individual circumstances.</p>

  <p>Generally, Canada <i>attempts</i> to operate a fair and equal tax regime. One of the primary ways this is achieved is through progressive tax rates. As a taxpayer makes more income, the rate of tax they pay increases. Income is divided into brackets, with each bracket taxed at progressively higher rates. There are two such brackets: one each for federal and provincial taxes. These brackets are combined and summarized for as below:</p>

  <p>As can be seen from above, individuals that make under ~$13k per year will not pay tax on their income.</p>
  <p>In addition, in Canada, only certain types of income are taxed. Some common types of taxed income include:
  <ul>
    <li>Employment income</li>
    <li>Self-employment income</li>
    <li>Certain employment benenfits</li>
    <li>Retirement income (i.e. from a pension)</li>
    <li>Certain amounts from an Registered Education Saving's Plan (RESP)*</li>
    <li>Interest income</li>
    <li>Dividends/Capital Gains**</li>
  </ul>
  <div class="box_warning">
	   <p><b>*Note:</b> While the original contributions to an RESP can be withdrawn tax-free, any grants from the government and all investment growth are taxable to the student, provided they are enrolled in school.   <p>If you have income, the organization that issued you the payment is also responsible for issuing you a tax slip. The <a href="https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/tax/individuals/topics/about-your-tax-return/tax-return/completing-a-tax-return/tax-slips.html"><i>CRA's website</i></a> contains sample slips. Generally, you will have recieved almost all of your tax slips by the end of March. Issuers are also required to electronically transmit slips to the CRA, who then makes the slips viewable electronically on your myAccount.</p>
